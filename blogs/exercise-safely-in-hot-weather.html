How to Exercise Safely in Hot Weather

Learn how to adjust exercise timing, intensity, clothing, hydration and expectations when training in hot or humid conditions.

How to Exercise Safely in Hot Weather

Training in heat feels harder because your body must support working muscles while also moving heat toward the skin. Heart rate can rise at a pace that normally feels comfortable, sweat losses increase, and a session that looked reasonable on paper may become too demanding.

The correct response is not to stop moving all summer. It is to adjust timing, intensity, clothing, hydration, and expectations. People with medical conditions, those taking medicines that affect heat tolerance, and anyone returning after illness should seek individual advice before training in high temperatures.

Check the conditions, not only the temperature

Temperature is only one part of heat stress. Humidity reduces the cooling effect of sweat because evaporation slows. Direct sun, low wind, dark surfaces, and protective equipment can increase the strain further. A shaded 28°C run may feel very different from the same temperature on exposed pavement.

Check the forecast, humidity, and UV Index before leaving. The World Health Organization recommends sun protection when the UV Index is 3 or higher. On very hot days, the NHS advises avoiding strenuous activity during the hottest part of the day and moving exercise to a cooler morning or evening period.

Reduce intensity before heat forces you to

Do not use your normal pace as the only measure of effort. Slow down and judge the session by breathing, perceived effort, and how steadily you can maintain form. A run may need to become a run-walk session. A hard cycling interval workout may need to become an easy aerobic ride.

Strength training can also be modified. Reduce the number of sets, extend rest periods, and avoid chasing personal records in an overheated room. The goal is to preserve useful training without creating unnecessary risk.

Acclimatisation takes repeated exposure

Your body can adapt to heat, but adaptation is gradual. The first hot week is not the time to prove toughness. Begin with shorter, easier sessions and increase exposure over several days. Athletes who have trained in cool conditions should expect a temporary drop in performance when the weather changes.

Do not assume that good fitness makes you immune. Experienced athletes can get into trouble because they are capable of producing more heat and may ignore early warning signs. Experience should improve judgement, not encourage denial.

Hydrate with a plan that avoids extremes

Arrive at the session normally hydrated. Drink regularly across the day rather than trying to compensate immediately before training. During long or very sweaty sessions, fluid and sodium needs vary with body size, sweat rate, intensity, and conditions.

For shorter moderate sessions, water is often sufficient. Longer sessions or heavy sweating may justify a drink containing electrolytes. Avoid forcing excessive amounts of water, which can also be dangerous. A practical approach is to learn how much weight you typically lose during similar sessions and replace fluids gradually afterward.

Wear clothing that helps heat escape

Choose light, breathable clothing that does not trap sweat unnecessarily. A cap can shade the face, but a thick or poorly ventilated hat may hold heat. For long outdoor sessions, consider clothing with good skin coverage and sun protection rather than relying entirely on sunscreen.

Apply broad-spectrum sunscreen to exposed areas, following the product instructions. The summer skincare routine for men explains how sunscreen fits into a simple daily system. Remember that sweating and towel drying can reduce protection.

Know the warning signs that end the session

Stop if you develop dizziness, confusion, unusual weakness, nausea, severe headache, loss of coordination, or chills despite the heat. Move to a cooler place, remove unnecessary clothing, and begin cooling. Heatstroke is a medical emergency, especially when confusion, collapse, or very high body temperature is present.

The NHS guidance on heat exhaustion and heatstroke recommends cooling the person and seeking urgent help when symptoms do not improve or when heatstroke is suspected. Do not leave an unwell person alone.

Use indoor alternatives without treating them as failure

On extreme days, substitute an air-conditioned gym session, a short mobility routine, or rest. One changed workout has almost no effect on long-term progress. Heat illness can disrupt training for much longer.

You can also split a session into two shorter blocks, reduce the warm-up because the environment is already warm, or choose shaded routes with access to water. Good programming responds to conditions rather than pretending they do not exist.

Finish with recovery, not a second challenge

Cool down gradually, move into shade, change out of soaked clothing, and replace fluids over the next several hours. Eat a normal meal containing carbohydrate, protein, and salt according to your needs. Monitor how you feel later in the day rather than assuming the risk ends when the workout stops.

Training in summer should build fitness, not test your willingness to suffer. Adjust early, stop when warning signs appear, and return to hard sessions when the conditions support them.
